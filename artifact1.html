CineMatch: An AI-Powered Movie Recommendation Chatbot

AIML-500 · AI Lab · Design Thinking + Generative AI Practice
→ Live CineMatch chatbot (Mizou)

Introduction

This artifact documents an end-to-end AI product exercise: designing, building, and testing a conversational movie recommendation chatbot using design-thinking methodology, alongside structured practice across four categories of generative AI tools, namely a base LLM, a custom GPT, an AI research assistant, and the CineMatch build itself.

Description

CineMatch is a conversational assistant, built on the Mizou platform, that recommends movies through natural back-and-forth dialogue rather than a static quiz or form. Before building it, I ran comparative practice across other AI tool types: an identical prompt across three LLMs (Gemini, ChatGPT, Claude), a custom GPT called Consensus (a research-synthesis tool), and STORM, a multi-perspective AI research assistant. Applying the define, ideate, prototype, test framework to CineMatch itself, I landed on a "conversational hybrid" design: a couple of guided questions followed by open-ended follow-up, because it balances structure with a natural feel.

Objective

To apply design-thinking methodology to a real AI product build, and to develop a working, evidence-based understanding of how different categories of generative AI tools behave differently on the same underlying task, a skill directly relevant to evaluating and selecting AI tools professionally.

Process

  • Defined the problem: reframed from "generate a list of movies" to "help someone quickly find something they'll enjoy without over-explaining."
  • Compared base LLMs: ran an identical Context/Task/Constraints prompt across Gemini, ChatGPT, and Claude. All three converged on Arrival, Ex Machina, and Sicario; Claude briefly self-corrected a release-date error, and ChatGPT was the only one to volunteer an extra "tier 2" list unasked.
  • Tested a custom GPT (Consensus) across six follow-up turns, including re-scoping, validating a stated opinion with evidence, and explaining what it deliberately excluded.
  • Tested a multi-agent research assistant (STORM), observing four simulated researcher personas interview simulated experts before synthesizing a cited article, then verified the output against a topic I already knew well.
  • Ideated three CineMatch designs (fixed quiz, fully conversational, hybrid) and selected the hybrid.
  • Prototyped in Mizou after Chatbase's onboarding required a website/URL knowledge source that didn't fit this use case, so I pivoted tools rather than forcing a workaround.
✓ PASS "I want a thriller" → bot asked a calibrating follow-up before recommending
✓ PASS "Something light and funny" (mid-convo pivot) → cleanly abandoned thread, re-asked for new mood
✓ PASS "I loved Parasite, what else?" → 3 recs, each reasoned against Parasite's tone
✓ PASS "I have seen all of them" → pivoted to deeper-cut tier, kept refining

Tools and Technologies Used

Mizou (chatbot builder), Gemini, ChatGPT, Claude, Consensus (custom GPT), STORM (AI research assistant), Microsoft Word (APA 7 documentation)

Value Proposition

This artifact shows I can take an AI concept from problem definition through a working, tested product, not just a written plan, and that I can evaluate AI tools critically across categories rather than treating them as interchangeable. That same evaluative instinct is what I already apply to choosing the right data tool or query approach in my SQL/dbt work.

Unique Value

Most portfolio pieces show either technical build skill or analytical comparison. This artifact shows both in one exercise: a shipped conversational AI prototype, tested against real scenarios, backed by a structured, evidence-based comparison of the AI tools that could power it.

Relevance

For Product Manager and AI-adjacent roles, the ability to prototype an AI-driven feature end-to-end, critically assess model behavior, and pivot when a tool's defaults don't fit the use case is the same judgment behind good product decisions: knowing when a feature, tool, or approach is the wrong fit and adjusting rather than forcing it, then being able to explain that decision clearly to both engineers and stakeholders.
